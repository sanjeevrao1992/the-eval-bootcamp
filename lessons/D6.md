## Part 1: The Concepts

### Why LLM judges work at all

A model-based grader uses an LLM to evaluate another LLM's output. At first, this seems circular — if the LLM makes mistakes generating answers, why would it be reliable at judging them?

The answer: **evaluating is easier than generating.** When your AI system generates an output, it has to understand the user's intent, apply policies correctly, handle edge cases, and produce a coherent result. That's hard. An LLM judge does something simpler — it looks at a completed output and checks whether it meets specific criteria. It doesn't need to produce a better answer, just assess the one in front of it.

Think of it like a restaurant critic versus a chef. A critic who can't cook a perfect soufflé can still reliably tell you whether the one on the table is good. Generating quality is harder than recognizing it.

This is why the same model family that produces flawed outputs can often reliably evaluate them — generating and evaluating are different cognitive tasks with different error profiles.

### The calibration trap: why vague rubrics produce confident wrong answers

The most common failure in LLM-as-judge is also the most tempting: writing a vague prompt like "Is this output correct? Rate its quality from 1 to 5" and trusting whatever comes back.

This fails in two specific ways.

**Problem 1: Multi-point scales are unreliable.** What's the difference between a 3 and a 4? Even human experts can't agree — studies consistently find inter-rater agreement (Cohen's Kappa) of 0.2 to 0.3 on Likert scales. If humans can't calibrate the difference between a 3 and a 4, an LLM judge won't either. The result: scores that look precise but carry no consistent meaning.

The fix: **use binary pass/fail.** A binary decision forces clarity. Either the output meets the criteria or it doesn't. No ambiguous middle ground. Leading industry practitioners converge on this point: binary labels lead to faster, more consistent evaluations from both humans and LLM judges.

**Problem 2: A single "God Evaluator" prompt tries to assess everything at once.** Faithfulness, relevance, tone, completeness, safety — all in one prompt. These multi-dimensional judges are impossible to calibrate because you can't isolate which dimension is misaligned when they disagree with humans.

The fix: **one judge per dimension.** Build separate judges for separate quality criteria. A faithfulness judge. A policy compliance judge. A safety judge. Each with its own rubric and few-shot examples. Then combine them: the output passes only if all relevant judges pass. Individual judges are simpler to build, easier to debug, and more flexible when criteria change.

### Critique Shadowing: the practitioner method for building reliable judges

You can't write a good judge prompt by sitting in a room and thinking about what "good" looks like. Research confirms **criteria drift** — people's standards shift as they see more examples. The criteria you'd write before reviewing data will be different from the criteria you'd write after.

The practitioner consensus is a process called **Critique Shadowing**:

**Step 1: Find your domain expert.** For a menu verification system, that's someone who knows the food delivery policies cold — probably a senior operations manager. Not a developer. Not a PM. The person whose judgment defines "correct."

**Step 2: Have the expert review 30-100 real outputs.** For each one, they make a binary pass/fail decision and write a **critique** — a detailed explanation of their reasoning. Not just "wrong" but "wrong because the 'wagyu' claim is unverifiable and no sourcing certificate is on file." These critiques are the secret ingredient. They externalize the expert's judgment criteria in a way that becomes learnable.

**Step 3: Build the judge prompt from the critiques.** Take the expert's pass/fail decisions and their critiques, and use them as few-shot examples in your LLM judge prompt. The judge learns to mimic the expert's reasoning — not from abstract rules, but from concrete examples of how the expert actually thinks.

**Step 4: Iterate until convergence.** Compare the judge's decisions to the expert's decisions on a held-out test set. Where they disagree, refine the prompt. In practice, teams typically achieve >90% agreement within three iterations.

The key insight: **you cannot build a reliable judge without first looking at the data.** The process of labeling examples is what teaches you what your criteria actually are. Skipping this step — going straight to "write me an eval prompt" — is the most common and most expensive mistake in LLM evaluation.

### Failure modes: how LLM judges go wrong

Even well-calibrated judges have systematic failure modes you need to watch for.

**Verbosity bias.** LLM judges tend to prefer longer, more detailed responses — even when the length adds no value or masks an incorrect answer. A wordy, confident reasoning paragraph that reaches the wrong conclusion will often get a PASS from a vague judge, while a terse but correct response might get a FAIL.

**Self-enhancement bias.** An LLM asked to evaluate output from an LLM of the same family may favor responses that match its own style and reasoning patterns. This is why using a different model for judging than for generating can be valuable.

**Position bias.** In pairwise comparisons (A vs B), judges tend to favor whichever response appears first. Mitigation: run the comparison twice with the order swapped. If the verdict flips, the outputs are too similar to distinguish — mark it as a tie.

**Criteria drift over time.** As your product evolves, the judge prompt stays static. New edge cases appear that the original rubric doesn't cover. A judge calibrated in January may be miscalibrated by June. Mitigation: periodic recalibration against fresh human labels — the same way you'd recalibrate any instrument.

**Domain knowledge gaps.** An LLM judge doesn't know your specific business rules unless you tell it. It doesn't know that "San Marzano" is a protected designation of origin, or that your company's policy treats allergen removal as a safety violation. These gaps produce systematic blind spots — the judge confidently passes things it should fail because it lacks the domain context.

### Meta-evaluation: judging your judge

Before trusting your LLM judge in production, you need to measure how well it performs. Treat it like any classifier and measure against the human ground truth:

**Agreement rate** is the starting point: what percentage of cases does the judge agree with the human? But raw agreement can be misleading when classes are imbalanced. If 85% of cases are PASS, a judge that always says PASS achieves 85% agreement while being completely useless.

**Precision on FAIL** answers the question: when the judge says something failed, how often is it actually a failure? Low precision means false alarms — your team wastes time investigating things the judge flagged incorrectly.

**Recall on FAIL** answers: of all the actual failures, what percentage does the judge catch? Low recall means the judge is missing real problems — which is dangerous.

For a production quality judge, **prioritize recall on FAIL.** It's better to flag something that turns out to be fine (a false alarm the team quickly dismisses) than to miss a real problem that reaches users. You can always triage false positives; you can't triage failures you never saw.

**Target:** aim for >80% agreement with human labels before scaling. In practice, if your LLM judge achieves higher recall and consistency than human annotators (who often agree with each other only 60-70% of the time), that's a success.

## Part 2: Exercise — Calibrating a Judge

Your team built an LLM judge for the menu verification system. They tried two versions of the judge prompt:

- **Judge V1:** A broad prompt — "Evaluate whether the menu verification decision is correct. Explain your reasoning, then give a verdict: PASS or FAIL."
- **Judge V2:** A specific rubric — "Check the following criteria: (1) For description changes, does the description add any unverifiable sourcing, origin, or premium ingredient claims without referenced certification? (2) Is the system's reasoning grounded in the data and established policy, or does it fabricate exceptions, cite non-existent rules, or make claims unsupported by the available evidence? Explain your reasoning against each applicable criterion, then give a verdict: PASS or FAIL."

Both judges evaluated the same 20 menu verification decisions. A domain expert also reviewed all 20 and provided ground truth.

### Setup

Open `exercises/judge-calibration-dataset.csv`.

Key columns: `case_id`, `change_type`, `change_summary`, `llm_decision`, `llm_reasoning` (what the menu verifier decided), `human_verdict` (PASS/FAIL ground truth), `human_critique`, `judge_v1_verdict`, `judge_v1_reasoning`, `judge_v2_verdict`, `judge_v2_reasoning`.

Each row is one menu verification decision, evaluated by both judge versions and the human expert.

### Step 1: Agreement rates

Calculate the agreement rate between each judge and the human: V1 vs human, V2 vs human. Which version is better calibrated?

### Step 2: Failure analysis of V1

Find the cases where V1 said PASS but the human said FAIL. Read the V1 reasoning alongside the human critique for each one. What pattern explains V1's blind spots?

### Step 3: How V2 improves

For the same cases you analyzed in Step 2, check whether V2 got them right. Compare V1 and V2's reasoning side-by-side on the same cases — what specific element of the V2 rubric caught the problem V1 missed?

### Step 4: Where V2 still fails

Find any cases where V2 also disagrees with the human — either PASS when human said FAIL, or FAIL when human said PASS. What does this tell you about the limits of even a well-designed rubric?
